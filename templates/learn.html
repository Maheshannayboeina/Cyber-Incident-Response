Learn About Crime Reporting

Explore resources to better understand crime reporting, your rights, and how to stay safe.

Why Report Incidents?

Reporting incidents is crucial for creating safer communities and digital spaces. It allows law enforcement to understand crime patterns, allocate resources effectively, and address the root causes of crime. By reporting, even seemingly minor incidents, you contribute to a more secure and just environment and help prevent future harm.

Understanding Different Types of Crimes

Being aware of different types of crimes helps you identify and report incidents accurately:

Theft

The unlawful taking of someone else's property, including physical possessions or digital assets.

Assault

Physical harm or threat of harm to another person, including verbal threats and acts of violence.

Fraud

Deceiving someone for financial or personal gain, including identity theft and scams.

Cybercrime

Crimes committed using computers, the internet, or other digital devices, encompassing hacking, malware, and online fraud.

Domestic Violence

Abuse within a family or household, including physical, emotional, and verbal abuse.

Your Rights as a Reporter

You have the right to report an incident without fear of retaliation. Depending on the nature of the crime, you can often report anonymously, and your information is generally kept confidential by law enforcement agencies to ensure your safety and the integrity of the reporting process. You also have the right to legal representation and protection throughout the process. Additionally, you are entitled to receive updates on the progress of the investigation, and have access to victim support programs.

How to Report an Incident

When reporting an incident, provide as many details as possible. Clear, accurate, and detailed information helps in effective investigation:

What happened?

Clearly describe the incident, providing specific details about what you experienced or witnessed, including the sequence of events.

Where did it happen?

Provide the location as accurately as possible, including street addresses, landmarks, or geographical coordinates. Note if it happened in a public or private place.

When did it happen?

Note the exact date and time of the incident, as this can be crucial in the investigation. If possible provide a time range if exact time is not known.

Who was involved?

Describe the individuals involved, if applicable, including physical traits, clothing, or other identifying information. Note if you saw any weapons or vehicles that can help the investigation.

Any evidence?

Note if you have any photos, videos, audio recordings, or other forms of evidence that might support your report. Note the type of evidence, and who has access to it.
